A.P.J Abdul Kalam

Great Indian Scientist and politician who played a leading role in the development of India's missile program and nuclear weapons

About the Legend

A.P.J Abdul Kalam, in full Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam, was born on October 15, 1931, in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, India.

He served as the 11th president of India from 2002 to 2007.

Kalam earned a degree in Aeronautical engineering from the Madras Institute of Technology and in 1958 joined the Defense Research and Development Organization (DRDO).

In 1969, he moved to the Indian Space Research Organization, where he was the project director of SLV-III, ht efirst launch satellite vehicle that was both designed and produced in India.

Rejoining DRDO in 1982, Kalam planned the program that produced a number of successfull missiles, which helped earn him the nickname Missile Man

India's first intermediate-range ballistic missile, which incorparated the basics of the SLV-III and was launched in 1989.

He also played a pivotal, organizational, technical, poltical role in India's Pokhran-II the original niclear test by India in 1974.

From 1992 to 1997 Kalam was Scientific Advisor to the Defense Minister, and he later served as Principal Scientific Advisor (1999-2001) to the government with the rank of cabinet minister.

His prominent role in the country's 1998 nuclear weapon tests solidifies India as a nuclear power and wstablished Kalam as a national hero, although the tests causedd gereat concern in the international cimmunity.

In 1998, Kalam put forward a countryside plan called Technology Vision 2020, which he described as a road map for transfroming India from a less-developed to a deveoped society in 20 years. The plan called for, among other measures, increasing agricultural productivity, emhasizing teechnology as a vehicle for econmic growth, and widening access to health care and education.
